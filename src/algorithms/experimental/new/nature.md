# Hegel on Love: A Marxian Recension

## On Sensuous Affirmation and Labor

If human feelings and passions are not merely anthropological phenomena but genuine ontological affirmations of being, and if they are only realized because their object exists for them as a sensuous object, then it is clear that:

1. These passions have many modes of affirmation, and the distinct character of their existence is constituted by the distinct mode of their affirmation. The manner in which the object exists for them is the characteristic mode of their gratification.

2. When sensuous affirmation involves the direct transformation of the object in its independent form—*as in eating, drinking, or laboring upon the object*—this is simultaneously the affirmation and negation of the object. **Here we see that human beings realize themselves through transforming nature; labor is the metabolic exchange between humanity and the natural world, and this productive activity is not merely consumption but the creation of the human world itself.**

3. Insofar as the human being is human, and not merely biological, the affirmation of the object by another is likewise their own gratification. **This reciprocal recognition occurs not in abstraction but through the social relations of production: we produce for one another, and in doing so, recognize each other as human.**

### On Production as Human Beings: The Structure of Unalienated Labor

**To understand what labor could be—what it is prevented from being under capitalism—Marx offers us this vision: Suppose we had carried out production as human beings. Each of us would have affirmed ourselves and the other person in two ways:**

**1) In my production I would have objectified my individuality, its specific character, and therefore enjoyed not only an individual manifestation of my life during the activity, but also when looking at the object I would have the individual pleasure of knowing my personality to be objective, visible to the senses and hence a power beyond all doubt.**

**Here labor is not the alienated drudgery of wage work but self-realization. The product does not confront me as alien property but as the objectification of my own powers. I see myself in what I have made. This is precisely what Hegel describes in love: the lover "senses life" in the beloved; each finds themselves reflected and confirmed in the other. In unalienated production, I find myself reflected in my product and, through it, in you who use it.**

**2) In your enjoyment or use of my product I would have the direct enjoyment both of being conscious of having satisfied a human need by my work, that is, of having objectified man's essential nature, and of having thus created an object corresponding to the need of another man's essential nature.**

**This is the social dimension of production. I produce not for abstract "exchange value" but for your concrete need. Your satisfaction is my satisfaction—not because I calculate some advantage but because your life and mine are bound together. This is the structure of love: "the affirmation of the object by another is likewise his own gratification." The lover takes pleasure in the beloved's pleasure; the producer takes pleasure in the consumer's satisfaction. Both transcend the egoistic calculation of bourgeois exchange.**

**3) I would have been for you the mediator between you and the species, and therefore would become recognized and felt by you yourself as a completion of your own essential nature and as a necessary part of yourself, and consequently would know myself to be confirmed both in your thought and your love.**

**Here is the key: in producing for you, I become essential to your life, and you recognize me as such. I am not merely an interchangeable vendor from whom you purchase a commodity; I am the specific person whose labor has satisfied your specific need. You know me, recognize me, confirm me. This is what Hegel means when he says that in love "the separate does still remain, but as something united and no longer as something separate." We remain distinct individuals, but each is a necessary moment in the life of the other. I am completed in you; you are completed in me.**

**4) In the individual expression of my life I would have directly created your expression of your life, and therefore in my individual activity I would have directly confirmed and realized my true nature, my human nature, my communal nature.**

**My individual activity is simultaneously social activity. There is no opposition between self-realization and the satisfaction of others. In producing for you, I realize myself; in realizing myself, I produce for you. This mutual constitution is identical to the structure of love. Hegel writes: "The lover who takes is not thereby made richer than the other; he is enriched indeed, but only so much as the other is. So too the giver does not make himself poorer; by giving to the other he has at the same time and to the same extent enhanced his own treasure." In love and in unalienated production, giving and receiving are not opposed but unified.**

**Marx concludes: "Our products would be so many mirrors in which we saw reflected our essential nature. This relationship would moreover be reciprocal; what occurs on my side has also to occur on yours."**

**This is the meaning of reciprocal recognition. Love and unalienated labor share the same structure: two living beings affirm themselves through affirming the other; each finds their own essence objectified in what they give to the other; each sees themselves mirrored in the other's satisfaction. The products of labor, like the gestures of love, become "mirrors in which we saw reflected our essential nature."**

### The Structural Identity of Love and Labor

**What Marx reveals is that Hegel's account of love describes the structure of all genuinely human activity. Love is not a private refuge from the alienation of labor; rather, love shows us what labor could be if it were not alienated. Both involve:**

- **Objectification without alienation**: I externalize myself in the object/the beloved, but the object/beloved remains *mine* in the sense that I recognize myself in it, not in the sense that I legally possess it.
- **Recognition through sensuous particularity**: The other recognizes me not as an abstract legal person or exchangeable worker but in my concrete individuality—my specific capacities, needs, personality.
- **Reciprocal constitution**: I realize myself through the other's realization; the other realizes themselves through mine. We are not self-sufficient monads but mutually dependent living beings.
- **The transcendence of property**: Neither love nor unalienated production can be structured by relations of exclusive possession. What I give you enriches me; what you give me enriches you.

**Under capitalism, labor is structured by the opposite principles:**

- **Alienation**: The product confronts the worker as alien property belonging to the capitalist.
- **Abstract recognition**: I am recognized only as "labor power," an exchangeable commodity, not in my concrete individuality.
- **Opposition of interests**: The capitalist's gain is the worker's loss; the wage is the price of surplus value extracted.
- **The reign of property**: All relations are mediated by ownership, contract, exchange value.

**This is why love under capitalism is simultaneously necessary and impossible. It is necessary because only in love can individuals experience the reciprocal recognition denied them in the workplace. It is impossible because the same individuals who love are also proprietors, embedded in property relations that structure them as isolated, competing egos. The private sphere of love is meant to compensate for the alienation of public labor, but it cannot escape the property relations that constitute both spheres.**

**The revolutionary implication: love cannot be fulfilled in private while production remains alienated. The transformation of production into genuinely human activity—production as mutual recognition, as reciprocal objectification, as the creation of each through the creation of all—is the precondition for love to become more than a temporary refuge. When our products become mirrors in which we see our essential nature reflected, when labor becomes the direct expression of life, then love ceases to be an exception to alienation and becomes the form of all human relations.**

## On Money: The Universal Confounding of Qualities

**Before examining how property fragments community, we must understand how money—the universal form of property—inverts the very structure of human individuality. For money does not merely mediate exchange; it transforms the essence of things and persons into their opposites.**

**That which is for me through the medium of money—that for which I can pay—that am I myself, the possessor of the money. The extent of the power of money is the extent of my power. Money's properties are my properties and essential powers. Thus, what I am and am capable of is by no means determined by my individuality.**

**I am ugly, but I can buy for myself the most beautiful of women. Therefore I am not ugly, for the effect of ugliness—its deterrent power—is nullified by money. I am lame, but money furnishes me with twenty-four feet. Therefore I am not lame. I am bad, dishonest, unscrupulous, stupid; but money is honored, and hence its possessor. Money is the supreme good, therefore its possessor is good.**

**What Marx describes here is the complete dissolution of the relationship between individual essence and social capacity. In a human community, what I can do flows from what I am; my powers are my actual powers; recognition depends on my concrete qualities. But money severs this connection. Under the regime of money, my powers are not mine but money's. I become what I possess rather than what I am.**

**This is what Marx means by money conferring "contradictory attributes upon their attributes": each quality of a person has its own essential property—ugliness repels, lameness restricts, stupidity prevents—but money inverts these properties into their opposites. Ugliness now attracts (because it comes with money), lameness now enables (because money buys transportation), stupidity now achieves (because money buys expertise). The attribute remains but its effect is reversed.**

**The implication for love is devastating. Hegel argues that true love is the recognition of the concrete individual: "life in the subject senses life in the object." I love you in your particularity, your specific qualities, your unique being. But if money transforms each quality into its opposite, then what am I loving? Am I recognizing *you*, or am I recognizing *money's power* as it manifests through you?**

**If I am ugly but money makes me attractive, then your attraction to me is not recognition of me but submission to money's power. If you are intelligent because you can purchase expertise, then my admiration of your intelligence is admiration of a commodity, not of a human capacity. Love under the rule of money is always already compromised—we cannot know whether we are recognized for ourselves or for our exchange value.**

**Money is the bond binding me to human life, binding society to me, connecting me with nature and man. But is not money also the bond of all bonds? Can it not dissolve and bind all ties? Is it not, therefore, the universal agent of separation? It is the coin that really separates as well as the real binding agent—the chemical power of society.**

**Here is the paradox: money appears to connect (I can buy access to you, to experiences, to things), but it actually separates (what I access is not really you but a commodity form of you; what we share is not genuine mutuality but a transaction). Money claims to be the universal medium of human relations, but it is actually the universal solvent that dissolves all immediate human connection into mediated exchange.**

**Shakespeare stresses two properties of money: (1) It is the visible divinity—the transformation of all human and natural properties into their contraries, the universal confounding and distorting of things: impossibilities are soldered together by it. (2) It is the common whore, the common procurer of people and nations.**

**The distorting and confounding of all human and natural qualities, the fraternization of impossibilities—the divine power of money—lies in its character as men's estranged, alienating and self-disposing species-nature. Money is the alienated ability of mankind.**

**That which I am unable to do as a man, and of which therefore all my individual essential powers are incapable, I am able to do by means of money. Money thus turns each of these powers into something which in itself it is not—turns it, that is, into its contrary.**

**For love, this means: if I long for union with you but lack the qualities that would make me lovable, money can purchase that union. It converts my wish from imagination into reality—but at the cost of making that reality unreal. I have purchased your presence but not your recognition; I have bought access to your body but not communion with your life. The demand based on money is "effective"—it gets results—but those results are alienated from genuine human need. I have you, but I do not have you; you are with me, but you are not with me. This is love in the form of the commodity: present but absent, intimate but distant, together but separated.**

**Money as the external, universal medium and faculty—not springing from man as man or from human society as society—transforms the real essential powers of man and nature into what are merely abstract notions and therefore imperfections and tormenting chimeras, just as it transforms real imperfections and chimeras into real powers and faculties.**

**In the light of this characteristic alone, money is thus the general distorting of individualities which turns them into their opposite and confers contradictory attributes upon their attributes.**

**This is why Hegel's vision of love—where "life in the subject senses life in the object," where recognition is immediate and unmediated, where "the lover who takes is not thereby made richer than the other"—is structurally impossible under capitalism. Money interposes itself between all subjects, making every recognition mediated by exchange value, every gift calculated as cost, every intimacy a transaction. We cannot sense life in the object when the object appears to us first and foremost as a bearer of price.**

## On Property, Alienation, and the Fragmentation of Community

The expansion of any collective whole—whether a religious community or cosmopolitan humanity—accompanied by the transposition of equality of rights into equality of *dependence*, diminishes individual dominion over objects. **This paradox reveals that under systems of private property, juridical equality masks real subordination. The "rights-bearing individual" is simultaneously the isolated, dependent individual, severed from control over the conditions of their existence.**

As each individual loses their worth, their pretensions, and their independence, they come to despise themselves—for their worth was measured by their share in dominion. **This is the self-alienation produced by the commodity form: human value appears as the value of things possessed, and when one possesses little, one appears as little. The legal form of property transforms human relations into relations between things, and things assume the properties of persons.**

Here there is no living union between the individual and their world; the object, severed from the subject, is dead. The only love possible is a relationship between the living subject and the dead objects by which they are surrounded. **This is commodity fetishism: the products of human labor confront their creators as alien powers, and social relations appear as relations between things. The worker faces their own product as something foreign, hostile, and independent.**

Since something dead forms one term of the love relationship, love is constrained by matter alone—matter indifferent to it. The essence of love at this level is that the individual is something opposed to objectivity; they are an independent unit for whom everything else is external. **This atomized individual—the legal subject of bourgeois society—is the product of definite historical relations of production. The apparent naturalness of this isolated ego is itself ideological: it universalizes what is specific to capitalist social forms.**

This attitude makes matter something absolute; but if the individual never existed, nothing would exist for them. What necessity was there for their existence? The eternal and self-complete unification with the object is lacking. **Yet this "lack" is not metaphysical but historical. Under communal forms of production, the individual exists immediately as a social being; their metabolism with nature is mediated by the community. The isolated individual emerges only when these communal bonds are dissolved—a process Marx calls "primitive accumulation," which tears people from their means of subsistence and transforms both labor and land into commodities.**

The individual cannot bear to think themselves in this nullity. They exist only as something opposed, and opposites are reciprocally conditioned. Subject and object, human and matter—each is only relatively necessary, existing only for the other, and hence existing in and for itself only through a power outside itself. **This externalized power is capital itself: the accumulated dead labor that dominates living labor. The individual's dependence on "alien Being" is their dependence on the valorization process, on the market, on the property relations that determine whether they eat or starve.**

Nowhere is independent existence found except in an alien Being—a Being which presents individuals with everything, whom they must thank for themselves and their immortality, for which they beg with fear and trembling. **This theological mystification has its material basis: the capitalist appears as the source of employment, the landlord as the source of shelter, the state as the guarantor of order. But these are inverted relations. It is labor that creates value; the capitalist merely owns the means of production. The real producers appear dependent on those who are actually dependent on them. This is the secret of commodity fetishism and the wage relation.**

## On True Love as the Overcoming of Opposition

True union, or love proper, exists only between living beings who are alike in power—living beings from every point of view, where neither is dead for the other. This genuine love excludes all oppositions.

Love is not understanding, whose relations always leave the manifold as manifold, whose unity is always a unity of opposites left as opposites. It is not reason either, because reason opposes its determining power to what is determined. Love is a feeling, yet not a single feeling among others. It is life present as a duplicate of itself and as a single unified self.

In love the separate does still remain, but as something united and no longer separate; life in the subject senses life in the object. **But this recognition cannot be sustained where property relations intervene. For under private property, the other appears not primarily as living being but as potential competitor, as excluder, as proprietor. The legal form freezes social relations into relations of right, transforming the fluid mutuality of love into the fixed antagonism of mine and thine.**

Love strives to annul even the distinction between the lover as lover and the lover as mortal organism, to annul the possibility of separation, to unite with itself even the mortal element and make it immortal. **Yet mortality here is not merely biological but social: the "mortal" is that which can be separated, divided, property-fied. Love struggles against the reduction of persons to bearers of property rights.**

If the separable element persists in either lover as something peculiarly their own before union is complete, it creates difficulty. There is antagonism between complete surrender—the cancellation of opposition in complete union—and subsisting independence. **This "independence" is not genuine autonomy but the pseudo-autonomy of the property owner, whose independence consists in excluding others from what they possess. Bourgeois independence is premised on dependence: on the market, on money, on the legal apparatus that enforces contracts and exclusion.**

Union feels independence as a hindrance; love is indignant if part of the individual is severed and held back as private property. **Here Hegel intuits the truth: private property is incompatible with genuine human union. The legal person—the rights-bearing subject—is necessarily a fragmented being, divided between their sensuous particularity and their abstract legal universality.**

## On the Child and Reproduction

What has been united in the child is not divided again; in love and through love, God has acted and created. This unity, however, is only a point, an undifferentiated seed. The lovers cannot contribute to it a manifold in itself at the start. The seed breaks free from its original unity, turns ever more to opposition, and begins to develop. Each stage of development is a separation, and its aim is to regain the full riches of life enjoyed by the parents. Thus: unity, separated opposites, reunion.

**Yet this biological reproduction occurs within definite social relations. The family itself is not a natural unit but a historically specific form, shaped by property relations. The bourgeois family, as Marx and Engels argued, is organized around the inheritance of private property; its structure reflects the need to transmit capital across generations. The oppression of women within the family corresponds to their status as transmitters of legitimate heirs—hence the preoccupation with female chastity and the legal subordination of wives. Love between man and woman is constrained by these economic imperatives. The child is not merely the fulfillment of love but the heir, the future proprietor.**

## On Property and the Impossibility of Love's Completion

After their union the lovers separate again, but in the child their union remains unseparated. This union can remain complete only as long as the lovers are opposed solely in the sense that one loves and the other is loved—that is, each is one organ in a living whole.

Yet the lovers are in connection with much that is dead; external objects belong to each of them. This means a lover stands in relation to things as objects and opposites, and lovers are capable of multiplex opposition in their acquisition and possession of property and rights.

**Here is the crux: property relations shatter the unity of love. The dead object in the power of one lover is opposed to both of them. To possess is to exclude, and exclusion is the negation of union. Where property exists, the other appears as a threat—they might lay claim to what is mine, they might negate my dominion. Even in love, the legal form of property reasserts itself, transforming partners into potential adversaries.**

The dead object in the power of one lover is opposed to both. A union in respect of it seems possible only if it comes under the dominion of both. The one who sees the other in possession of property must sense in the other the separate individuality which has willed this possession. **This is the tragedy of love under conditions of private property: the beloved appears not only as beloved but as proprietor, as a separate will that excludes. One cannot simply annul the other's exclusive dominion without opposing the other's power—and this opposition of wills is the very thing love seeks to overcome. Thus property makes genuine love impossible.**

One cannot annul the exclusive dominion of the other, for this would be an opposition to the other's power; one would be opposing a mastery to the other's dominion and canceling the other's relationship—their exclusion of others from their property.

**This contradiction cannot be resolved within the framework of private property. Even community of goods—shared legal title—only extends the right of possession to both parties; it does not abolish the legal form itself. The thought of right would never be forgotten, because everything humans possess has the legal form of property. Two proprietors are still proprietors, still structured by relations of exclusion toward the rest of the world, still defined by the abstract universality of legal right rather than the concrete particularity of living need and desire.**

## Conclusion: Toward the Abolition of Private Property

**The implication is clear, though Hegel draws back from it: genuine love—the complete union of living beings in which neither is dead for the other—requires the abolition of private property. As long as human relations are mediated by the ownership of things, by the legal forms of contract and right, by the commodity form and the wage relation, love remains incomplete, broken, alienated. The lovers themselves become property in relation to each other—spouses in bourgeois marriage exchange rights over one another's bodies and labor.**

**Marx would go further: the overcoming of alienation requires not merely the communal sharing of property between lovers but the revolutionary transformation of the social relations of production. Only when the means of production are held in common, when labor is no longer wage labor, when products are no longer commodities, when the legal form of private property is abolished—only then can human beings recognize one another not as proprietors but as living, sensuous, social beings. Only then does love cease to be a private refuge from alienation and become the organizing principle of social life itself.**

**The unity-opposition-reunion that Hegel describes dialectically must be understood historically and materially. The original unity is not paradise lost but primitive communism; the opposition is class society, private property, the state; the reunion is communism—not a return to the past but a higher synthesis that preserves the technological and organizational achievements of class society while abolishing its exploitative relations. In that future, love will no longer struggle against property, because there will be no property to struggle against. Humans will produce for one another not as isolated proprietors but as associated producers, and the free development of each will be the condition for the free development of all.**
